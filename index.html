Overview: Six Ethical Frameworks

About This Analysis

üèõÔ∏è Virtue Ethics

Assumptions about Agency

Virtue ethics views human beings as rational animals (Aristotle's term) whose moral life centers on character and practical wisdom. Agents are assumed to have innate tendencies and emotions that, when properly nurtured, yield stable virtues. Virtues integrate reason and feeling ‚Äì e.g., courage or compassion are dispositions shaped by both intellect and motivation. A virtuous agent deliberates in light of phronesis (practical wisdom), aiming to flourish.

Epistemology

Moral knowledge comes from habituation, exemplars, and moral perception. Virtue ethicists emphasize learning from role models and experience. "Moral education and development is a major part of virtue ethics," relying on emulation and practice. In other words, one knows how to act by cultivating virtuous habits and seeing how a good person behaves. Abstract principles play a secondary role: virtue ethics asks "How should I live?" rather than applying universal rules.

Ontological Assumptions

Virtue ethics typically assumes a teleological human nature ‚Äì that there is an objective human good (eudaimonia or flourishing) grounded in our function. For Aristotle, our distinctive function is reasoning well, so the good life is one of rational activity. Thus values like human flourishing or excellence are treated realistically as intrinsic ends. The self is understood as a developing character, and objective human goods exist even if realized in particular ways by different people.

Source: Virtue Ethics | Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy

‚öñÔ∏è Consequentialism

Assumptions about Agency

Consequentialist theories (e.g., utilitarianism) assume agents are rational maximizers of aggregate good. They presuppose that individuals can impartially weigh outcomes and act to maximize overall value. Emotions or particular attachments are usually downplayed; the focus is on agents' capacity to evaluate consequences. The typical utilitarian assumes agents can predict and compare the effects of actions: "moral agents are sufficiently rational to accurately predict the consequences of their actions."

Epistemology

Moral knowledge in consequentialism is formed by forecasting outcomes and calculating net good. The theory relies on an act-calculus: one must know or estimate the pleasures, pains, costs, and benefits caused by each action and aggregate them. In practice this epistemology can be demanding: it assumes we can identify and measure all relevant consequences (often a very complex inference). Thus, moral reasoning is often treated as a kind of utility calculation.

Ontological Assumptions

Consequentialism generally entails a realist value ontology: it posits one or more objective goods that outcomes can embody. Classic utilitarianism, for instance, holds that pleasure (happiness) is the only intrinsic good. In this view the world has a measurable property ‚Äì total happiness or welfare ‚Äì which grounds right action. Consequentialists thus assume the world contains countable values (e.g., units of well-being) that can be summed or compared. Agents and actions have no value apart from their effects on these objective goods.

Sources: Consequentialism (Stanford Encyclopedia) | Consequentialism and Utilitarianism (IEP)

üìú Deontological Ethics

Assumptions about Agency

Deontological frameworks (especially Kantian) posit that agents are autonomous rational legislators. Humans are viewed as ends-in-themselves whose rational will can discern universal laws. Kant, for example, assumes a "kingdom of rational agents" bound by reason. Agency here means acting from duty, not inclination: an agent's own rational capacity generates the binding moral law. Emotional impulses or contingent motivations are subordinated to duty.

Epistemology

Moral knowledge is a priori: it is discovered by reason itself. In Kantian terms, we know our duties by applying the Categorical Imperative, a test of universalizability, through pure reason. There is no reliance on empirical consequences or social conventions. One deduces obligations from rational consistency or respect for persons. Knowledge of right action comes from rational reflection on principles, not from sensory observation or tradition.

Ontological Assumptions

Deontology assumes objective moral laws and rights exist independently of human opinion or benefit. These duties are metaphysically grounded (in some accounts by the fact that all rational wills must legislate the same laws). Values such as justice, dignity, and autonomy are treated as metaphysically real. The nature of the self is that of a free rational agent with intrinsic worth. This view presumes a robust moral realism: ethical facts (e.g., "one must not lie") are taken as universally binding, not reducible to psychology or culture.

Source: Rationality - Seven Pillars Institute

ü§ù Care Ethics

Assumptions about Agency

Care ethics starts from the premise that humans are relational and vulnerable. Agents are seen as embedded in networks of dependency and care. Unlike theories built on abstract autonomy, care theorists assume people are motivated by empathy and concern for others. The moral agent is one who recognizes and responds to the needs of dependents; caring feelings and emotions (love, compassion) are central. Autonomy is de-emphasized in favor of interdependence.

Epistemology

Care ethics bases moral knowledge in contextual, empathetic understanding. Knowing what to do comes from listening to concrete voices and relationships rather than applying impartial rules. It emphasizes narratives and particular situations: one learns how to care by cultivating sensitivity to others' needs. Thus reasoning is less deductive and more narrative or "story-like." There is no single calculable metric; moral insight is grasped through embodied experience and responsiveness.

Ontological Assumptions

Care theory implies a relational ontology. The self is not an isolated atom but fundamentally connected to others. Identities and values arise within the web of relationships and social roles. Care ethics generally rejects atomistic individualism and universalism: moral value is grounded in specific relationships (parent-child, friend, community). It does not posit abstract universal values, but views moral obligations as flowing from the facts of dependency and the importance of caring connections.

Source: Care Ethics | Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy

ü§úü§õ Social Contract Theory

Assumptions about Agency

Social contract views assume individuals are rational, self-interested (or impartial) agents who enter agreements. Early contractarians (Hobbes) depict humans in a state of nature as self-interested and rationally seeking self-preservation. Later versions (Rawls) assume agents are equally rational and impartial behind a veil of ignorance. In all cases, agency is modeled on bargaining and consent: agents can understand the game and form mutual agreements. Emotional or non-rational motivations are backgrounded in favor of reasoned cooperation.

Epistemology

Morality is known via hypothetical agreement and rational deliberation. Agents derive principles by imagining fair bargaining situations (e.g., Rawls' original position) or consenting to basic rules that serve all. This is a kind of constructivism or contractualism: moral norms are justified to the extent that all rational agents would agree to them under specified conditions. Knowledge is thus reached through reflective reasoning about fairness and mutual advantage, not through divine command or sentiment.

Ontological Assumptions

Contract theory typically treats moral values as derivative of mutual consent rather than metaphysically given. The content of morality depends on the form of the agreement. For example, Rawls' principles of justice are "chosen" under fair conditions. Some contractarians allow a thin realism (the "right" can be defined as what free and equal agents would accept). In general, it denies transcendent moral facts; instead, moral obligations exist to the extent that rational agents endorse them. There is a basic realism about justice and mutual recognition, but only insofar as they are outcomes of the contract.

Source: Social Contract Theory | Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy

üåê Coherence-Based Thermodynamic Ethics

Assumptions about Agency

Coherence Ethics conceives moral agency in thermodynamic/informational terms. An agent's capacity to act ethically is seen as an achievement of low-entropy coherence. It assumes human agents require a stable biological substrate, predictable institutions, coherent narratives, and a unified phenomenological horizon in order to function as deliberative beings. When these conditions degrade (e.g., under stress or social chaos), agency collapses. Thus agency is not simply rational volition; it depends on environmental and physiological factors that maintain identity and order. Emotion and cognition are important only insofar as they contribute to or erode this coherence.

Epistemology

Moral knowledge is treated functionally, not metaphysically. Coherence Ethics uses a kind of abductive, predictive inference: agents model what actions will preserve coherence. In this view, "normativity becomes a functional necessity, not a metaphysical postulate." Rather than invoking duty or utility, one asks "What preserves low-entropy stability of identity and relationships?" The framework provides algorithms (e.g., predictive salience, coherence-based inference) to identify such actions. Thus moral reasoning is akin to pattern recognition under uncertainty, aligning actions by their systemic effects.

Ontological Assumptions

Critically, Coherence Ethics assumes NO shared ontology. It is explicitly ontology-light and pluralistic. Every agent interprets "coherence" and meaning through a personal Phenomenological Reference Frame (PRF) ‚Äì which could be religious, secular, indigenous, scientific, etc. ‚Äì and the theory makes no claim that any agent must adopt a common metaphysical view. The formal structure (e.g., "Coherent Information Bits" and Identity Kernel) is universal, but its interpretation is flexible. In practice, this means values are not fixed: a theist may think of coherence as spiritual integrity while a humanist thinks of it as resilience. Thus Coherence Ethics treats moral facts as relative to coherence-preserving functionality. It avoids realists' heavy commitments by treating normative coordination as a matter of functional equivalence ‚Äì different reasons or worldviews can lead to the same coherence-enhancing actions.

Key Innovation

Under conditions of information scarcity or high "entropy," the ontology-light Coherence approach offers practical coordination: it aligns actions by their functional effect (stability, trust, identity coherence) rather than by contested reasons. Agents working from different PRFs can still achieve functional equivalence‚Äîfor example, both a religious and a secular agent might build trust in their community‚Äîbecause the formalism does not depend on their personal rationales.

Understanding the Ranking

Frameworks are ranked from most minimal (fewest metaphysical/epistemological assumptions) to most demanding (requires accepting many contested claims about reality, reason, and values).

Minimal Commitments

Explicitly ontology-neutral; it imposes only a formal coherence constraint, not any substantive worldview. It relies on agents' own PRFs (Phenomenological Reference Frames) and aligns actions by functional effect. Different metaphysical beliefs can lead to the same coherence-preserving actions.

ü§ù Care Ethics

Light Commitments

Avoids grand metaphysical postulates, focusing on concrete human relations and emotions. Its assumptions about caring motivation and vulnerability are considered uncontroversial and context-dependent. No need for abstract universal values.

Assumes idealized rational agents and often a background notion of justice or mutual advantage. These are abstract but still relatively defeasible (e.g., critics point out limitations on who is included). Constructivist rather than realist about values.

üèõÔ∏è Virtue Ethics

Stronger Commitments

Posits an objective human good (flourishing) and assumes certain human functions (like rationality). While less rigid than formal laws, it still requires realism about human nature and character. Assumes there is a teleological end for humans.

‚öñÔ∏è Consequentialism

High Commitments

Presumes a measurable, aggregable value (e.g., total happiness) and that agents can compute outcomes. This demand for an objective utility scale and far-reaching calculation is metaphysically and epistemologically heavy. Requires strong rationality assumptions.

üìú Deontological Ethics

Most Demanding

Requires a robust rationalist ontology: universal, a priori moral laws (such as Kant's Categorical Imperative) that bind all agents. It presumes idealized reasoners and neglects empirical variance, making very deep claims about human nature and moral reality. Most metaphysically ambitious.

Practical Implications

Under conditions of information scarcity or high "entropy," the ontology-light Coherence approach offers practical coordination: it aligns actions by their functional effect (stability, trust, identity coherence) rather than by contested reasons. Agents working from different PRFs can still achieve functional equivalence because the formalism does not depend on their personal rationales.

In contrast, frameworks with heavier assumptions (like deontology or consequentialism) may fail when agents disagree on fundamental metaphysical or rational principles, making coordination more difficult in diverse or high-stress environments.
